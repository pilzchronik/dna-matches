Overall Analysis of the FamilyTreeDNA Results

Wolfgang Pilz · Autosomal (Family Finder), Y-DNA and mtDNA — with a platform comparison to MyHeritage and Ancestry · Status: 13 June 2026 · FTDNA kit 330595 · Y-DNA J-FT159612 (J2b2-L283) · mtDNA H41a9 · YFull YF66621

← Back to the DNA overall synthesis

1 Summary in plain words

FamilyTreeDNA is the richest of the three platforms: it tests not only the autosomal DNA (all lines) but additionally the pure paternal line (Y-DNA) and the pure maternal line (mtDNA), and is the only one to offer a chromosome browser with which shared DNA stretches can be located exactly and assigned to several relatives at once (triangulation). The essentials without jargon:

The paternal line is genetically very rare — and a genealogical dead end

Your direct paternal line belongs to haplogroup J-FT159612, an extremely rare branch (only a handful of tested carriers worldwide). The root is old and Balkan–Southeast European — unusual for a Saxon-Erzgebirge peasant line. In practice this means: there are no close paternal-line cousins. The nearest common ancestor lies about 2,000 years back. For documentary research the Y-DNA therefore brings nothing new, but it is a fascinating deep finding.

The maternal line is clearly East Tyrol — with a Slavic primal substrate

The mtDNA (haplogroup H41a9) leads seamlessly to East Tyrol (Kartitsch) and shows as its nearest match a person of Croatian origin — evidence of the Alpine-Slavic substrate of the region, not a contradiction.

The real added value: the segment map

Via the chromosome browser the autosomal matches can be sorted into five segment clusters (A–E). Among them is the known Zeisl finding (cluster B), but also an important new, NON-Jewish line of its own (cluster E: H. J. and Solbrig) that points to the Erzgebirge Kreissl line — something MyHeritage and Ancestry cannot show at all for lack of segment data.

2 Data basis and methodology

For kit 330595 FamilyTreeDNA lists about 3,079 autosomal Family Finder matches and 231 Y-DNA matches (35 of them Big-Y). This evaluation relies on the FTDNA raw data archived in the project (Y-DNA match list, chromosome-browser segment data) and the segment-cluster analysis already carried out, supplemented by the Y/mtDNA findings from FTDNA and YFull.

Data status: the FTDNA data was captured live from the logged-in account on 13 June 2026: origin estimate (myOrigins v3), the Family Finder top matches (3,085 matches in total) as well as Y-DNA (231 matches) and mtDNA. Supplemented by the already documented chromosome-browser segment evaluation.

Unlike MyHeritage and Ancestry, the chromosome browser is central at FTDNA: it allows a shared segment to be assigned to several people and thus to form genuine triangulation groups — the decisive step from “shares DNA” to “traces back to a common ancestor”.

Important caveat on myOrigins v3: FTDNA's origin model is the coarsest of the three platforms and partly misleading. The 40 % “England, Wales & Scotland” is NOT to be read as British ancestry — for lack of a suitable reference FTDNA often assigns Central European DNA to the West European–British cluster. Together with the 44 % “West Slavic” this simply describes the German-Austrian-Bohemian core that MyHeritage (46 % Germanic) and Ancestry (59 % South Germanic) resolve more finely.

Sephardic Jewish 4 %: FTDNA's label for the Jewish share (the Theresia line). The same order of magnitude as MyHeritage 1.5 % / Ancestry 3 % — all three small and consistent with a single Jewish ancestor. The term “Sephardic” is model-driven. Genealogically this is Ashkenazi origin.

South Caucasus <3 % + Sardinia 4 %: remnants that fit the deep J2 paternal line (Southeast European–West Asian root) — not a younger line of its own.

Conclusion on origins: despite different labels FTDNA shows the same basic picture as the other two platforms — dominantly Central European, small Jewish share. The Alpine southern-side/Tyrol component that MyHeritage and Ancestry clearly indicate is, however, not resolved separately by FTDNA v3.

5 Y-DNA — the direct paternal line (J-FT159612)

Y-DNA is inherited only from father to son and shows the male Pilz stem alone. Result: terminal haplogroup J-FT159612 (chain J2b2 → L283 → … → FT161874 → FT159612), a very rare branch.

Rarity: only a handful of Big-Y carriers of this terminal branch worldwide. Of 231 Y matches none is within genealogical reach — the nearest (Parfenov, Mordovia, 20/630 STR differences) sits on a different branch (J-Y33541).

Deep origin: TMRCA of the terminal branch about 1,950 years (YFull). Rare ancient connections point to a Roman-era Balkan, Gothic and medieval Croatian trail — Southeast European, not typically Central European.

Closest real relative (YFull): József Csuzdi (Hungary, ~1760), same terminal branch J-Y183338 — but the common ancestor is ~1,950 years back, i.e. beyond any documentary genealogy.

Countries of origin of the Y matches (male-stem spread): broadly distributed across Europe with a striking Balkan/Southern Europe component (Albania, Serbia, Greece, Georgia among the closest), plus Germany, Italy, British Isles. This fits the deep J2 root, not a younger Central European line.

Finding: a J2 paternal line in a Saxon-Erzgebirge peasant family is highly unusual and supports the hypothesis of an outside immigration into the Pilz line at some point before ~1770. The Y-DNA is not usable genealogically for lack of close cousins — it remains a quiet but remarkable deep finding.

6 mtDNA — the direct maternal line (H41a9)

mtDNA is inherited only from the mother and shows the female stem alone. Result: haplogroup H41a (H41a9).

Documentary line: seamlessly to East Tyrol up to Katharina Krambl (*1695, Kartitsch/Lienz), peasant-Catholic, eight generations.

Closest full-sequence match: Richard Dale Shively (genetic distance 2), maternal origin Croatia. The common ancestor lies before 1695.

Assessment: the Croatia signal fits the Carantanian-Alpine-Slavic substrate of East Tyrol (the place name “Windisch-Matrei” = Slavic). The maternal line is Alpine-Slavic assimilated. It thus stands independently behind the 25.9 % “North Italian” (MyHeritage) and 10 % Northeast Italy + 4 % Slovenia (Ancestry) and the community “Slovenia/Northwest Croatia”. Important: the Zeisl relationship does NOT run via the maternal line — that line is closed off within East Tyrol.

7 What FTDNA additionally reveals: the non-Ashkenazi lines of its own

FTDNA's greatest substantive added value over MyHeritage and Ancestry is cluster E on chromosome 13 (52–74 Mb) — a line of its own, NON-Jewish, that becomes visible only thanks to segment data:

H. J. (kit 340888, iGENEA): 31.9 cM total (23.1 cM on Chr 13 + 8.8 on Chr 10) — statistically the closest of the evaluated single matches. Y-DNA R-L2 (a Central/West European R1b branch, NOT Ashkenazi). Working hypothesis: belongs to the Christian Central European side, most likely via the paternal Erzgebirge line (Emma Hofmann) or the maternal Alpine line.

B Solbrig: 17.1 cM in exactly the same Chr-13 range (52–74 Mb) — confirms the triangulation candidate. In Solbrig's own Ancestral Surnames stands “KREISSL, Czechoslovakia”. The Erzgebirge connection is thereby half-confirmed: the family tree contains two Kreissl women (Maria Theresia Kreissl *1793 Krásný Les; Maria Anna Kreissl *~1720 Kovářská).

Significance: cluster E is the first solid indication of a non-Ashkenazi DNA line of its own in the 17–32-cM range, parallel to the Zeisl complex. It points to the Erzgebirge Kreissl line. This is exactly the kind of finding only a platform with a chromosome browser can deliver.

Caveat: the Kreissl connection is made plausible via Solbrig's surname entry, but not proven documentarily at segment level (degree of speculation: working hypothesis). ICW and matrix checks in FTDNA are pending.

Core statement: FTDNA complements the other two platforms in three places they cannot cover at all: the paternal line (Y-DNA J-FT159612), the maternal line (mtDNA H41a9) and segment-precise triangulation. The most important new insight from the segment data is the non-Jewish line of its own (cluster E, Erzgebirge/Kreissl) — a strand that the pure match lists of MyHeritage and Ancestry do not resolve.

9 Open questions and recommendations

Check In-Common-With for the new clusters A (Bernstein/Levine) and D (Meisel Zager/Lager) — this decides whether they are genuine triangulation groups.

Deepen cluster E: cross-check H. J. (iGENEA) and B Solbrig in the matrix tool. Trace the Kreissl line (Maria Theresia Kreissl *1793) genealogically.

Complete the Fisher brothers Chr 14: repeat GEDmatch One-to-One I↔Herbert and add Lester Fisher to the chromosome browser (third replication).

Set up parent phasing: link a related person (e.g. on the Pilz site) in the family tree to the kit — then FTDNA automatically buckets all 3,085 matches paternal/maternal, which greatly eases line assignment.

Y-DNA: no further action needed — for lack of close cousins it remains a background finding (writing to matches would be futile).
