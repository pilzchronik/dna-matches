Overall Analysis of the MyHeritage DNA Results

1 Summary in plain words

This report evaluates the entire MyHeritage DNA profile and places it in the context of the other tests (FamilyTreeDNA, GEDmatch, YFull). The most important statements first, without jargon:

What the origin estimate shows

Nearly half of the DNA (46 %) is assigned by MyHeritage to a German-Austrian-West Bohemian area — which fits exactly the paternal line from the Bohemian Erzgebirge (Vejprty/Weipert, Kovářská). A quarter (26 %) is “North Italian”: not a contradiction but the genetic fingerprint of the maternal line from the tri-border area East Tyrol–South Tyrol–Trentino. The Jewish share is only 1.5 %.

The apparent contradiction in the Jewish share — resolved

Strikingly many Jewish (Ashkenazi) relatives appear in the match list, although the origin estimate shows only 1.5 % Jewish DNA. This is not an error. The 1.5 % corresponds almost exactly to what a single Jewish ancestor about six generations back leaves arithmetically (about 1/64 ≈ 1.56 %). This ancestor has a name in my family tree: Theresia/Pessel, who converted to Christianity in Amschelberg in 1768. That so many Jewish matches nonetheless appear is due to “endogamy”: Jewish families married almost exclusively among themselves for centuries, which is why even a small share of Jewish ancestry produces very many small, hard-to-date matches.

The two confirmed family lines among the top matches

Maternal (Tyrol): the strongest match overall — Daniela Webhofer, 94.7 cM — is a proven 2nd cousin via the Reider line from Kartitsch/Sexten. At least six further top matches demonstrably belong to this Tyrolean family circle.

Paternal (Bohemia): Oliver Bechinie von Lazan (44.9 cM) is a proven cousin via the Bohemian Bechinie line. The common ancestor is Josef Bechinie (*1776). Through him the dense Jewish circle of relatives (Theresia) is also explained.

What is new and still open

The second-strongest match in the entire database — Frédéric Macé from France, 84.6 cM — has so far been recorded in no research note and has no family tree on file. He has already been contacted — so far without response; deferred for now. A Norwegian match too (Abel Slyngstad, 47.5 cM) and a group of Czech and Hungarian matches remain unresolved so far.

In short: the MyHeritage data independently confirms what the parish registers and the other DNA tests show — a Tyrolean maternal and a Bohemian paternal line with a small but clearly localisable Jewish branch. Newly added are some strong, not yet placed matches.

2 Data basis and methodology

The basis is the live data read from my MyHeritage account on 13 June 2026: the ethnicity estimate, the assigned Genetic Groups, the Ancient Origins report structure and the match list sorted in descending order by shared DNA.

MyHeritage shows a total of 11,204 DNA matches. For this analysis I systematically recorded the top 259 matches (down to 25 cM of shared DNA), of which the top 88 with full detail data (number of segments, largest segment, country of origin). This limit is professionally justified: in Ashkenazi-influenced data sets, single matches below about 20–25 cM can usually no longer be assigned to a concrete, genealogically tangible person, but are an expression of population relatedness (see section 5).

Assessment yardstick: the relationship degrees shown by MyHeritage (e.g. “3rd cousin”) are usable for the non-endogamous Alpine matches, but systematically too close for the Jewish matches — endogamy makes two people appear more closely related than they are genealogically. Relationship degrees derived from cM values are therefore given only with a range and a caveat.

3.1 Interpretation of the main components

Germanic (46.2 %): covers the paternal Bohemian-Erzgebirge Pilz line (Vejprty/Weipert, Kovářská, Karlovy Vary area; Saxon roots before 1770) as well as the general Austrian background. The assigned Genetic Groups (section 4) confirm exactly this area.

North Italian (25.9 %): strikingly high and at first sight in need of explanation. The resolution is confirmed: the maternal Reider line sits in the border area East Tyrol–South Tyrol–Trentino (Kartitsch, Sexten, Pustertal). MyHeritage genetically assigns this Alpine southern side to the North Italian cluster. The strong maternal match cluster (section 6.1) independently confirms this assignment.

East European (14.6 %) and Balkan (5.2 %): partly Czech-Moravian background, partly attributable to the Ashkenazi share (the MyHeritage model regularly mixes East and Southeast European components into Ashkenazi DNA). The Balkan share is compatible with the deep paternal Y-DNA signal (J2, Balkan-Gothic trail), but not to be equated with it — a direct assignment would be speculation.

Ashkenazi Jew / Central Europe (1.5 %): the quantitatively smallest but genealogically most precisely localisable share. See the separate appreciation in section 6.3.

English (2.7 %), Dutch (2.6 %), Dane (1.3 %): small Northwest European remnant components. At this low level such values are common in Central European profiles and not necessarily attributable to a concrete line.

3.2 Finding: the Jewish share is consistent with a single ancestor

Theresia/Pessel carries the Kekulé number 69 in the family tree. A position in the range 64–127 corresponds to the seventh generation, i.e. an ancestor six meioses before me. The statistical expected value of the DNA contribution of such a single ancestor is (1/2)^6 = 1/64 ≈ 1.56 %. The measured Ashkenazi share of 1.5 % thus matches almost exactly.

Assessment (not proof, but strong consistency): the value is exactly what would be expected if Theresia/Pessel is the only Jewish source in the family tree. Note the high variance of inheritance at this depth — a single value does not prove sole sourcing, but it does not refute it either and fits seamlessly into the documentary picture.

5.2 Segment profile — the decisive dividing line

More important than the raw cM height is the segment pattern. Among the 259 recorded matches:

39 matches share a single large segment (≤ 2 segments, largest ≥ 22 cM) — this is the signature of real, more recent common descent (IBD). This is exactly where the Alpine maternal matches belong (e.g. Albertini 35.3 cM on 1 segment, Slyngstad 30.6 cM).

69 matches share many small segments (≥ 4 segments, largest < 13 cM) — the classic endogamy signature. They cluster among the US-American and French matches and are not individually datable.

Practical consequence: a match with 35 cM on one segment is genealogically more valuable than one with 40 cM on six segments. The prioritisation of contacting should be guided by the segment pattern, not by the cM total alone.

6 Cluster analysis of the meaningful matches

Status: confirmed (shared-match test 12 June 2026).

By far the strongest match in the entire database, Daniela Webhofer (94.7 cM / 5 segments / largest 26.5 cM), is the granddaughter of Maria Webhofer née Reider, a sister of my grandmother. This gives a proven relationship as 2nd cousins with the common ancestor Leonhard Reider zu Peterer (*1854). At least six further top matches demonstrably triangulate via Daniela:

Robin Debelle (#3, 73.7 cM, Belgium) — same branch as Webhofer

Gabriele Seidl (#4, 55.3 cM) and Florian Seidl (#10, 46.2 cM)

Via the Pustertal sub-branch (Köck family) the following are additionally linked: Gabriela Köck (#12, 45.6 cM, Italy/South Tyrol) — proven as 4th cousin once removed, common ancestor Franz Reider zu Äußerst (*1783 Sexten) — as well as Josef Köck (#29), Josef Moser (#50) and Josef Pfeifhofer (#6, 51.0 cM, connected to Köck at 92.9 cM).

Onomastically belonging, but NOT confirmed: Tilo Albertini (#14), Michael Da-Pozzo (#8) and Daryl Tschurtschenthaler (#35) carry Alpine names but do not triangulate with Daniela Webhofer in the test. They can be assigned to the Alpine area by name, but are not documented for the specific Reider branch.

Cross-confirmation: this cluster independently explains the 25.9 % “North Italian” of the ethnicity estimate.

6.2 Paternal cluster — Bechinie (Bohemia)

Status: confirmed (triple-convergent evidence, 12 June 2026).

Oliver Bechinie von Lazan (#13, 44.9 cM / 3 segments / largest 21.5 cM, Germany) is a true cousin via the paternal Bohemian Bechinie line. The common ancestor is Josef Bechinie (*1776) ∞ Marianna Handl. The bridge runs via his children Marie (my line) and Franz (emigrated to Hungary, Oliver's line). The cM value is consistent with both “4th cousin” and “3C1R” — what is fixed is the common ancestor, not the exact number of steps.

Important negative finding: Oliver's 432 shared matches form a dense Ashkenazi cluster (via Theresia). In the comparison, however, none of the MyHeritage top 25 shares DNA with Oliver at the same time. The previously assumed assignment of the East-Central European cluster (Kovalovská, Barcza, Pluskota, Damari) to the Bechinie/Theresia line is thereby

refuted and discarded. These matches share DNA with me, but not with Oliver — their line is open.

Note: the match “Samuel Schreiber” (#23) is NOT identical with the “Schreiber” from Oliver's shared-match list (that is “Andreas Schreiber née Ullrich”, a different person).

6.3 Ashkenazi background — Theresia/Pessel and the Zeisl complex

The Jewish share of 1.5 % traces back to Theresia/Pessel, who converted to Catholicism in Amschelberg (Košová Hora) in 1768 and came from Miskowitz/Myslkovice (South Bohemia). The DNA connection to the Jewish world runs not via Theresia herself (she left the Jewish marriage network) but via collateral relatives who remained Jewish.

The strongest evidence of this line lies not on MyHeritage but on GEDmatch: the match Walter Steven Zeisl (36.57 cM total, including one contiguous segment of 29.1 cM on chromosome 9). On MyHeritage the same Ashkenazi world shows up in breadth — the many US, French and Israeli matches with a small-segment profile. Some of these matches are recognisable across platforms:

Reva Jacobson Kimble (#81, 30.8 cM): very probably corresponds to “Reeva K./Kimble” of the GEDmatch chromosome-9 small-segment group (origin Łomża/Mir; discarded as MRCA, but a group member).

Arlene Rosalind Goodstein (#37, 35.2 cM, 1 segment): “Goodstein” also appears among Oliver's Ashkenazi shared-match cluster — a candidate for the Theresia line. On 17 June 2026 confirmed via the shared-match level as a shared match with Oliver Bechinie (see Addendum 6.5).

Professional caveat: for Ashkenazi matches in the 7–30 cM range the common ancestor may lie 300–1,000 years back. Such matches are usually not genealogically manageable and must not be misunderstood as a “close cousin”, even if MyHeritage labels them that way.
