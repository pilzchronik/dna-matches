Diagrams & Visualisations

Five figures as a visual companion to the DNA analysis

This page brings together five self-contained diagrams that make the Pilz–Zeisl DNA findings visible — from broad ancestry down to a single chromosome segment. They are deliberately plain and use their own cool visual style, set apart from the book and from the rest of this site.

Two reading aids: a solid line marks a documented, evidenced connection; a dashed line a hypothesis or a genetically secured but documentarily still-open bridge. All figures are our own research findings or estimates (GEDmatch/FTDNA, 2026), not primary sources — each diagram states its own status.

1 · Where the DNA comes from

Overall ancestry in two halves — paternally Bohemian, maternally Alpine — with ranges across the three providers and a clear note on the model uncertainty of ethnicity estimates.

2 · The DNA connection to Walter Zeisl

Two documented lines and the genetic bridge between Wolfgang Pilz and Walter Steven Zeisl — 36.57 cM, the strongest match.

3 · Zeisl — three roots, one open bridge

Three separate Zeisl roots and Wolfgang's line: the connection runs through DNA, not through documents. Guiding principle — the same surname is not the same as a shared ancestor.

4 · Chromosome 9 — shared segments

The shared DNA segments around the Zeisl line on chromosome 9 — the small segment and the 29.1 cM large segment at opposite ends, plus the triangulation core.

5 · Four ancestral lines to the Zeisl family

How the 43 shared matches spread across four separate ancestral lines — plus the chromosome-9 large segment — and what triangulation does (and does not) prove here.
