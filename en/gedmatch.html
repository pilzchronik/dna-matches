Overall Analysis of the GEDmatch Results

Wolfgang Pilz · Cross-platform matches, triangulation and ancestor reconstruction — with comparison to MyHeritage, Ancestry and FTDNA · Status: 13 June 2026 · GEDmatch kit QU4148768

← Back to the DNA overall synthesis

1 Summary in plain words

GEDmatch is not a testing company but an open comparison platform: people upload their raw data from all providers (23andMe, Ancestry, MyHeritage, FTDNA, Living DNA and others) here to compare them across companies. That makes GEDmatch the project's analytical toolbox. The essentials without jargon:

One match clearly stands out: Zeisl

Walter Steven Zeisl, at 36.6 cM (including one large contiguous piece of 29.1 cM on chromosome 9), is by far the strongest GEDmatch match — all the others lie at 22–28 cM and are mostly anonymous aliases from the Ashkenazi sphere. That one large segment is the proof of a real relationship (not random noise) and the core of the entire Jewish trail (Theresia/Myslkovice).

The real strength is the tools

GEDmatch allows three things the testing companies do not offer: the exact single comparison of two kits (One-to-One), triangulation (assigning one segment to several relatives at once) and Lazarus reconstruction (genetically rebuilding a long-deceased ancestor from several relatives). With these, three relationship groups were confirmed and a “Myslkovice ancestor” was reconstructed.

GEDmatch also corrected errors

Several previously circulating, too-high cM figures (such as “272 cM” for Zeisl or “~200 cM” for the reconstructed ancestor) turned out to be phantom numbers when checked against the exact GEDmatch comparisons and were corrected to the documented values. That is a gain in reliability.

2 Data basis and methodology

The basis is the One-to-Many match list retrieved live from the GEDmatch account (kit QU4148768) on 13 June 2026, together with the GEDmatch analyses documented in the project: One-to-One Q-matching evidence, the Tier-1 triangulation and the Lazarus runs. Data source and privacy: All DNA matches named here are publicly visible to logged-in users on the respective platforms and are reproduced for research and documentation purposes (see Privacy).

What makes GEDmatch special: it compares across companies. The “Source” column in the match list shows which database a kit comes from — 23andMe, MyHeritage, FTDNA, Genotek, HAPI-DNA, Ancestry, Living DNA. This reveals relatives who would never appear together at any single company.

Methodological yardstick: as on the other platforms, for Ashkenazi-influenced matches in the 7–30 cM range the common ancestor may lie centuries back. GEDmatch's generation estimate (the “Gen” column) is systematically too optimistic in this range. A match only becomes meaningful through a large single segment (as with Zeisl) or through triangulation.

4 The Zeisl finding (One-to-One Q-matching)

The exact single comparison between me and Zeisl (documented 19 Apr 2026) yields:

Large segmentChr 9: 76.36–95.82 Mb = 29.1 cM (4,843 SNPs)

Why this is a real relationship (IBD), not endogamy noise: a contiguous segment of 29.1 cM with 4,843 SNPs lies well above the threshold. Endogamy shows up as many small pieces (3–7 cM), not as a single large one. Zeisl is thus a concentrated Chr-9 match — the genetic anchor of the Jewish line (Theresia/Pessel from Myslkovice).

5 Triangulation — three confirmed groups

Triangulation means: three or more people share the SAME segment and therefore trace back to a common ancestor. Three groups were confirmed via GEDmatch (Tier-1):

TG9_0 (Chr 9 large) is the main finding: GEDmatch automatically set the triangulation group for Zeisl ↔ Cohen ↔ Jovings (core overlap 87.6–92.2 Mb). I share the same zone — all trace back to a common South Bohemian Jewish ancestor.

Two paths converge in me: the Chr-9 group (Zeisl line) and the Chr-2 group (petctdoc/Renee Fisher) do NOT triangulate with each other — they are two independent inheritance paths that overlap in me.

Caveat: three of the five core members of the small Chr-9 group (Kimble, Cohen, petctdoc) locate their origin in the Pale of Settlement (Belarus/Ukraine), not in Bohemia. The segments are genuine IBD, but the common ancestor lies deeper in the Ashkenazi past than first assumed.

6 Lazarus — the ancestor reconstruction

Lazarus partially rebuilds the genome of a common, long-deceased ancestor from the DNA pieces of several living relatives. For the hypothetical “Myslkovice ancestor” the pseudo-kit DR227221L2 was created (277.3 cM reconstructed).

My comparison with the pseudo-kit: 108.4 cM / 3 segments. I contribute about 39 % of the reconstruction — I am thus a dominant descendant of this reconstructed ancestor.

Important self-correction: a 74-cM segment on Chr 2 in the reconstruction turned out to be an artefact — no real pair comparison (neither with petctdoc nor with Fisher) shows this segment. Lazarus had assembled haplotype fragments into a virtually oversized block. My real contribution is closer to ~34 cM (Chr 9) plus small pieces.

Caveat (degree of speculation): the term “Myslkovice ancestor” is a working title for the common ancestor of the large Chr-9 cluster, NOT a proven place assignment. The DNA triangulation is confirmed; the specific place (Myslkovice vs. another South Bohemian village) remains a historical hypothesis.

8 What GEDmatch delivers beyond the other platforms

Cross-company comparison: matches from 23andMe, Ancestry, MyHeritage, FTDNA, Living DNA, Genotek, HAPI-DNA in one list — relatives who would otherwise never appear together on any single platform.

One-to-One with an adjustable threshold: exact segment coordinates and cM for any kit pair comparison.

Triangulation (Tier-1): the step from “shares DNA” to “common ancestor”.

Lazarus: partial genetic reconstruction of deceased ancestors — available on no testing company.

In the overall picture of the four platforms: MyHeritage and Ancestry provide the maternal Alpine top (Reider line), FTDNA provides Y/mtDNA and the segment clusters, GEDmatch provides the precise Zeisl anchor and the triangulation/reconstruction tools. Only together do they form the complete picture.
