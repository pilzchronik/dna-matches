Overall Analysis of the AncestryDNA Results

Wolfgang Pilz · Autosomal matches, origins and communities — with a platform comparison to MyHeritage · Status: 13 June 2026 · AncestryDNA kit B33DB807 · companion document to the MyHeritage overall analysis

1 Summary in plain words

This evaluation concerns the AncestryDNA account and complements the separate MyHeritage analysis. The essentials first, without jargon:

The maternal Tyrolean line is the strongest finding here too

By far the strongest Ancestry match is Ursula Webhofer with 123 cM. She is the sister of Daniela Webhofer, who was the top match on MyHeritage — both belong to the maternal Reider line from East Tyrol. That the same family stands at the very top on both platforms (through two different sisters) is a very strong, double confirmation of this line.

An important warning about Ancestry's automation

Ancestry tries to assign every match automatically to the paternal or maternal side. This automation is demonstrably faulty for your kit: Ursula Webhofer was originally assigned to the paternal side, although the line is clearly maternal. The side indication is therefore treated in this analysis only as a non-binding hint and never used as proof.

Origins: consistent with the known history

About 59 % of the DNA is South German–Austrian–West Bohemian (paternal Erzgebirge line and Austrian background), 10 % North Italian and 4 % Slovenian (the maternal Alpine line and its Alpine-Slavic substrate), 3 % Ashkenazi Jewish (the Theresia line). The three Ancestry communities — Austria/Southeast Slovenia, Northeast Italy, Slovenia/Northwest Croatia — confirm exactly the maternal Alpine region.

What is open

The second-strongest match, Cris Haas (98 cM), has neither a family tree nor a side assignment and is completely unresolved — the most important new Ancestry lead (the counterpart to Frédéric Macé on MyHeritage).

2 Data basis, methodology and a central caveat

The basis is the live data read from the AncestryDNA account on 13 June 2026: the origin estimate (as of October 2025), the communities and the match list sorted by relationship. Ancestry shows about 4,600 matches (231 pages of 20 each). The top 100 matches down to 16 cM were recorded. Below that begins the endogamy range that can no longer be evaluated individually.

Unlike MyHeritage, Ancestry offers no chromosome browser and no segment data. A segment-profile analysis as on MyHeritage is therefore not possible here. The assessment relies on cM total, family trees and shared matches.

⚠ Central caveat — Ancestry's parent-side assignment (SideView) is demonstrably wrong for this kit.

4 Communities (Genetic Communities)

Ancestry assigns three origin communities — locating the ancestors of the last ~300 years:

Austria and southeastern Slovenia — with the tree-linked ancestor Stefan Eberstaller (6×-great-grandfather, †1722). Matches the mixed paternal-maternal Austrian origin.

Northeast Italy — the southern Alpine side of the maternal Reider line.

Slovenia and Northwest Croatia — coincides with the Alpine-Slavic substrate of the maternal mtDNA line (Croatian top match, historically explained as the Carantanian-Slavic heritage of East Tyrol).

Assessment: the communities are more place-precise than the percentage estimate and independently support the maternal Alpine line. The linked Eberstaller ancestor is a concrete, documentarily traceable anchor.

5 The overall picture of the Ancestry matches

With ~4,600 matches Ancestry counts considerably fewer than MyHeritage (11,204). This is due to the database and to higher thresholds — Ancestry reports small endogamy segments less often as individual matches. The top drops off steeply: after Webhofer (123 cM) and Cris Haas (98 cM), the third match already follows at 53 cM, and from about rank 14 everything is below 25 cM.

Parent-side distribution of the top 100 (subject to caveat, see section 2)

Of the top 100 matches Ancestry assigns 8 to the maternal and 10 to the paternal side; the remaining 82 stay unassigned. Given the documented error, this distribution is only roughly indicative — the matches marked “paternal” in particular must be checked independently.

Only a single top-100 match (Webhofer) has a common ancestor recognised by Ancestry. About half have a linked family tree at all.

6 Analysis of the meaningful matches

6.1 Maternal line — Reider, doubly confirmed across platforms

Status: confirmed.

Ursula Webhofer (123 cM, 2 % shared DNA, linked family tree, common ancestor recognised by Ancestry) is the sister of the MyHeritage top match Daniela Webhofer. Both descend from the maternal Reider line (East Tyrol, Kartitsch/Sexten). The common ancestor with Wolfgang is Leonhard Reider zu Peterer (*1854), the relationship 2nd cousins. That on two platforms a different sister of the same family forms the strongest match is an exceptionally robust confirmation.

Further probably maternal-Alpine matches (via name/region, not via the — unreliable — side automation): Maria Tippett (46 cM, tree with 71 people), Andrea Jacobs (24 cM, tree 153), Heidi Vergeiner (22 cM — Vergeiner is an East Tyrolean name), Philip Oberstaller (17 cM — Oberstaller likewise East Tyrol). These are to be noted as research targets for the Reider line, but not yet confirmed via shared matches (working hypothesis).

6.2 Paternal line — more weakly represented on Ancestry

The paternal anchor confirmed on MyHeritage (Oliver Bechinie, Bohemia) has no obvious counterpart at the top on Ancestry. That is expected: Bohemian and Czech lines are thinly represented in the Ancestry database. The strongest match marked “paternal” by Ancestry is DeanGVaeth (49 cM, a very large tree with 1,321 people) — but not confirmed as paternal because of the SideView caveat. The large tree nonetheless makes it a worthwhile target to check.

Note (speculation): among the weaker matches is Edward Taussig (18 cM). “Taussig” is a well-known Bohemian-Jewish family name and could belong to the Theresia/Zeisl complex — unconfirmed.

6.3 Ashkenazi background

The 3 % share and individual names in the match list (e.g. Jeffrey Cohen, Molly Levy, Simon Glaser, Carol Kaye) reflect the Theresia/Pessel line. As on MyHeritage: these matches are mostly not datable genealogically because of Ashkenazi endogamy. The common ancestor may lie centuries back. The quantitatively strongest evidence of this line remains the Zeisl match on GEDmatch, not an Ancestry match.

6.4 Unresolved top match — Cris Haas (98 cM)

At 98 cM, Cris Haas is the second-strongest Ancestry match overall, but has neither a tree nor a side assignment. 98 cM points to a relatively close relationship (around the 3rd-cousin range). This is the Ancestry counterpart to the unresolved MyHeritage match Frédéric Macé (84.6 cM) — whether it is the same line or person is open.

→ highest contact priority on Ancestry.

8 Open questions and recommendations

Cris Haas (98 cM) has already been contacted — no response; deferred for now. Strongest unresolved match. A possible connection to Frédéric Macé (MyHeritage) remains open.

Check DeanGVaeth (49 cM, tree with 1,321 people) — large family tree, worthwhile target. Do NOT adopt the side assignment unchecked.

Verify Alpine surname bearers: connect Maria Tippett, Andrea Jacobs, Vergeiner, Oberstaller to the Reider line via shared matches.

Note the Taussig lead as a possible Bohemian-Jewish connection (unconfirmed).

Distrust SideView on principle: independently re-check the parent assignment for every match used (documented error case Webhofer).
