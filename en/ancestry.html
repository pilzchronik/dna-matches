Overall Analysis of the AncestryDNA Results

Wolfgang Pilz · Autosomal matches, origins and communities — with a platform comparison to MyHeritage · AncestryDNA kit B33DB807 · companion document to the MyHeritage overall analysis

← Back to the DNA overall synthesis

1 Summary in plain words

This evaluation concerns my AncestryDNA account and complements the MyHeritage analysis. The essentials first, without jargon:

The maternal Tyrolean line is the strongest finding here too

By far the strongest Ancestry match is Ursula Webhofer with 123 cM. She is the sister of Daniela Webhofer, who was the top match on MyHeritage — both belong to the maternal Reider line from East Tyrol. That the same family stands at the very top on both platforms (through two different sisters) fits exactly the family-known Reider line.

An important warning about Ancestry's automation

Ancestry tries to assign every match automatically to the paternal or maternal side (SideView). For my kit this automation originally assigned Ursula Webhofer to the paternal side, although the line is clearly maternal; I corrected the label by hand. SideView is therefore not reliable — I never use the side indication as proof.

Origins: consistent with the known history

About 59 % of the DNA is South German–Austrian–West Bohemian (paternal Erzgebirge line and Austrian background), 10 % “North Italian” and 4 % “Slovenian” (genetic labels of the maternal Alpine line — German-speaking East Tyroleans (origins a side-valley away in South Tyrol), not Italian/Slovenian heritage), 3 % Ashkenazi Jewish (the Theresia line). The three Ancestry communities — Austria/Southeast Slovenia, Northeast Italy, Slovenia/Northwest Croatia — confirm exactly the maternal Alpine region.

What is open

The second-strongest match, Cris Haas (98 cM), has no tree of his own, but via DeanGVaeth's tree he sorts into a probably paternal Bartl-Bohemia lead (working hypothesis) — the most important new Ancestry lead (counterpart to Frédéric Macé on MyHeritage).

2 Data basis, methodology and a central caveat

The basis is the live data read from the AncestryDNA account on 13 June 2026: the origin estimate (as of October 2025), the communities and the match list sorted by relationship. Ancestry shows about 4,600 matches (231 pages of 20 each). I recorded the top 100 matches down to 16 cM. Below that begins the endogamy range that can no longer be evaluated individually.

Unlike MyHeritage, Ancestry offers no chromosome browser and no segment data. A segment-profile analysis as on MyHeritage is therefore not possible here. The assessment relies on cM total, family trees and shared matches.

⚠ Central caveat — Ancestry's parent-side assignment (SideView) was originally wrong for my kit. Ancestry first assigned Ursula Webhofer (strongest match, clearly maternal Reider line) to the paternal side — evidently wrong; I corrected the display to maternal by hand. The automation itself remains unreliable: it rests on few phasable matches and, of its own accord, reclassifies the line as “paternal” — not the algorithm self-healing, just my overridden display. I therefore carry the side column only for information (marked with ¹) and never count it as proof of maternal/paternal; the line assignment is done exclusively via known anchors, family trees and shared matches.

4 Communities (Genetic Communities)

Ancestry assigns three origin communities — locating the ancestors of the last ~300 years:

Austria and southeastern Slovenia — with the tree-linked ancestor Stefan Eberstaller (6×-great-grandfather, †1722). Matches the mixed paternal-maternal Austrian origin.

Northeast Italy — the southern Alpine side of the maternal Reider line.

Slovenia and Northwest Croatia — coincides with the Alpine-Slavic substrate of the maternal mtDNA line (Croatian top match, historically explained as the Carantanian-Slavic heritage of East Tyrol).

Assessment: the communities are more place-precise than the percentage estimate and independently support the maternal Alpine line. The linked Eberstaller ancestor is a concrete, documentarily traceable anchor.

5 The overall picture of the Ancestry matches

With ~4,600 matches Ancestry counts considerably fewer than MyHeritage (11,204). This is due to the database and to higher thresholds — Ancestry reports small endogamy segments less often as individual matches. The top drops off steeply: after Webhofer (123 cM) and Cris Haas (98 cM), the third match already follows at 53 cM, and from about rank 14 everything is below 25 cM.

Parent-side distribution of the top 100 (subject to caveat, see section 2)

Of the top 100 matches Ancestry assigns 8 to the maternal and 10 to the paternal side; the remaining 82 stay unassigned. Given the documented error, this distribution is only roughly indicative — the matches marked “paternal” in particular must be checked independently.

Only a single top-100 match (Webhofer) has a common ancestor recognised by Ancestry. About half have a linked family tree at all.

6 Analysis of the meaningful matches

6.1 Maternal line — Reider, at the top on both platforms

Status: confirmed.

Ursula Webhofer (123 cM, 2 % shared DNA, linked family tree, common ancestor recognised by Ancestry) is the sister of the MyHeritage top match Daniela Webhofer. Both descend from the maternal Reider line (East Tyrol, Kartitsch/Sexten). The common ancestor with me is Leonhard Reider zu Peterer (*1854), the relationship 2nd cousins. That on two platforms a different sister of the same family forms the strongest match fits exactly the family-known Reider line. On Ancestry, however, the otherwise ~50-strong Tyrolean cluster does not replicate — Ursula Webhofer has only a single shared match there (Carmel Gutierrez, 25 cM, no tree); Alpine lines are thin in Ancestry's holdings (database effect, not a contradiction).

Further probably maternal-Alpine matches (via name/region, not via the — unreliable — side automation): Maria Tippett (46 cM, tree with 71 people), Andrea Jacobs (24 cM, tree 153), Heidi Vergeiner (22 cM — Vergeiner is an East Tyrolean name), Philip Oberstaller (17 cM — Oberstaller likewise East Tyrol). These are to be noted as research targets for the Reider line, but not yet confirmed via shared matches (working hypothesis).

6.2 Paternal line — more weakly represented on Ancestry

The paternal anchor confirmed on MyHeritage (Oliver Bechinie, Bohemia) has no obvious counterpart at the top on Ancestry. That is expected: Bohemian and Czech lines are thinly represented in the Ancestry database. The strongest match marked “paternal” by Ancestry is DeanGVaeth (49 cM, a very large tree with 1,321 people) — but not confirmed as paternal because of the SideView caveat. The large tree nonetheless makes it a worthwhile target to check. This very tree provides the Bartl-Bohemia handle (see 6.4).

A second, independent paternal-tending cluster forms around Frank Teichert (53 cM, no tree): his shared matches — Erica Sheibeneker (34 cM), Ronald Risenhoover (27 cM), Jason Risenhoover (25 cM), dasaridaki (12 cM, tree of 14,505 people) and Rolf Lange (9 cM) — overlap neither with the Cris Haas cluster nor with Webhofer (US names Risenhoover/Sheibeneker). dasaridaki's tree is a worthwhile pool to check despite only 12 cM.

Note (speculation): among the weaker matches is Edward Taussig (18 cM). “Taussig” is a well-known Bohemian-Jewish family name and could belong to the Theresia/Zeisl complex — unconfirmed.

6.3 Ashkenazi background

The 3 % share and individual names in the match list (e.g. Jeffrey Cohen, Molly Levy, Simon Glaser, Carol Kaye) reflect the Theresia/Pessel line. As on MyHeritage: these matches are mostly not datable genealogically because of Ashkenazi endogamy. The common ancestor may lie centuries back. The quantitatively strongest evidence of this line remains the Zeisl match on GEDmatch, not an Ancestry match.

6.4 Top match Cris Haas (98 cM) — probably paternal (Bartl-Bohemia)

At 98 cM, Cris Haas is the second-strongest Ancestry match, but has no tree of his own; 98 cM points to a relatively close relationship (around 3rd cousin). Via the shared-match level he nonetheless sorts into place: his four shared matches — DeanGVaeth (49 cM, tree of 1,321 people), jdstas78 (45 cM), Kerstin Nishen (11 cM) and R. L. (10 cM) — form a core with DeanGVaeth and jdstas78, without Webhofer/Tyrol. The decisive point is DeanGVaeth's tree: it carries a Josef Bartl *1875 Bohemia (†1956; ∞ Barbara Hanzlik 1870–1941, place of death Broadview Heights/Ohio; daughter Rosa Bartl Spacek 1895–1983). “Bartl” is one of my paternal family names (one of several paternal lines).

Hence the working hypothesis: Cris Haas as a probably paternal relative via a Bartl-Bohemia line. Caveat: “Bartl” is a common Bohemian-German name — the connection must be verified against the documented Bartl ancestors via concrete persons and places, not by the name alone. 98 cM with only four shared matches fits a Bohemian line thinly represented in Ancestry. Highest contact priority; a possible connection to Frédéric Macé (MyHeritage) remains open.
