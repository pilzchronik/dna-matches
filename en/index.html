Why This Page Exists

My name is Wolfgang Pilz. For years I have been researching the history of my family using traditional genealogical methods — church records, parish registers, archives. The Pilz Chronicle documents this work across centuries and multiple regions.

In 2019, I additionally took an autosomal DNA test and uploaded the results to GEDmatch to compare them against a broader database. The DNA confirmed much of what the paper sources already showed — and it revealed something that had become almost invisible in the church records: a component of Ashkenazi Jewish ancestry.

This component has a name in my family tree: Theresia (Kekulé No. 69), born as Pessel in Myslkovice, Bohemia. She converted from Judaism to Catholicism in 1768 and thereby disappeared from the Jewish marriage network. Almost nothing was previously known about her Jewish family.

This is exactly where DNA comes in. Among my matches, one particularly strong hit stood out: the Zeisl family from Vienna — a Jewish family with roots in the same Bohemian region. The following chapters document the systematic analysis of this discovery — from the DNA results through the family trees to the open questions.

1. Background — The Zeisl Match

On GEDmatch, Wolfgang Pilz and Walter Steven Zeisl share 43 common matches. A single segment of 29.1 cM on Chromosome 9 confirms a real genealogical connection — not endogamy noise.

The Core Puzzle: How are the Pilz and Zeisl families related? According to MRCA calculation (4.3 generations), the common ancestor likely lived between 1830 and 1870. The connection probably runs through relatives of Theresia who remained in Bohemia’s Jewish marriage network after her conversion in 1768.

Y-DNA of the Zeisl Family — A Conversion Finding

Research by Zeisl descendants revealed another remarkable finding: Walter Steven Zeisl's Y-DNA shows a match with Peter Caizl in Brno, whose line traces back to a Zeisel who was already Catholic by the mid-18th century. Since the Y-DNA line is otherwise consistent with a deep Jewish background, a conversion from Judaism to Christianity must have taken place at that time.

Significance for surname research: This finding proves that the surname Zeisel already existed as a Jewish name before the Tolerance Edict of Emperor Joseph II (1782) mandated the adoption of fixed surnames. A conversion in the mid-18th century falls in the same period as Theresia/Pessel's conversion from Judaism to Catholicism in 1768 — two independent conversions in the same Bohemian-Moravian region.

The closest geographical reference point of the Zeisl family to Košová Hora was long considered to be Koloděje nad Lužnicí (cf. Places of Origin, Section 7: Stampfer/Lewy family). Since the discovery that a Zeisl family is also documented in Miskowitz (Myslkovice, ~45 km southeast), this location now takes precedence as the nearest reference point (cf. Places of Origin, Section 7).

7. Places of Origin of Zeisl Ancestors

The Familiants Laws (Familiantengesetze, 1726–1849) forced marriages between Jewish communities in Bohemia and Moravia. Family heads were allowed to marry only one child (usually the eldest son) in their ancestral community; other sons and daughters had to emigrate. This led to large-scale marriage networks.

New finding: Miskowitz / Myslkovice (Tábor District, South Bohemia)
Jewish community with ghetto (since late 17th c.), 37 Familiants, synagogue since 1770.
Significance: Miskowitz is the first location where the place of origin of a direct ancestor of the author and a residence of the Zeisl family geographically overlap:
  • Pessel/Theresia (Kekulé 69, b. c. 1735) originated from Miskowitz and married into Amschelberg/Košová Hora c. 1764.
  • Zeisl family documented in the 1869 census (house XVI) — first concrete evidence of a geographic overlap between Pessel's place of origin and a Zeisl family residence.
  • Bloch family also documented in the same census (house 56) — the name appears in the Zeisl pedigree (Rosalia Bloch, *1852 Čkyně).

Geographic Pattern: Three clusters observable — Central/East Bohemia (Zeisl/Reichmann around Rozsochy, Pardubice), South Bohemia-West (Feitler/Bloch/Stampfer around Budweis, Rožmberk) and South Bohemia-East (Miskowitz near Tábor — place of origin of Theresia/Pessel, and simultaneously residence of the Zeisl family in the 19th c.). The South Bohemia connections explain the proximity to Košová Hora.

9. Excursus: Connection to Arnold Schönberg

           

Erich Zeisl's daughter Barbara married Ronald Schönberg, son of composer Arnold Schönberg (1874–1951, founder of twelve-tone technique). Their son is E. Randol Schönberg, the internationally recognized attorney in the Klimt case ("Woman in Gold"). Randol Schönberg is active on Geni.com and co-founder of JewishGen Austria-Czech SIG.

           
                Erich Zeisl (1905–1959): The composer emigrated in 1938, first to London, then to Los Angeles. His work Requiem Ebraico is a classic of Jewish exile music. Archive: Exilarte.            
       

In April 2026, the family confirmed their willingness to collaborate on researching the shared connection. A comparison of family trees is in preparation.

           

10. Excursus: Hermine Dannhauser née Zeisl — Innsbruck

           

Hermine Dannhauser (née Zeisl, *1875 Jungbunzlau / Juniper) and her daughter Philipine Dannhauser (*1903 Innsbruck) plus other family members were deported from Innsbruck to Sobibor in 1941 and murdered there.

           

The Dannhauser family was part of Innsbruck's Jewish community. Witness: Berta Dannhauser (98 years old) survived Kristallnacht on November 9, 1938.

           

Research Note: This connection is a coincidence (the researcher lives in Innsbruck), not directly genealogically connected to the Pilz-Zeisl line. However, it illustrates the fate of the Bohemian Jewish diaspora.
