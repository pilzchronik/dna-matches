Why This Page Exists

My name is Wolfgang Pilz. For years I have been researching the history of my family using traditional genealogical methods — church records, parish registers, archives.

In 2019, I additionally took an autosomal DNA test and uploaded the results to GEDmatch to compare them against a broader database. The DNA confirmed much of what the paper sources already showed — and it revealed something that had become almost invisible in the church records: a component of Ashkenazi Jewish ancestry.

This component has a name in my family tree: Theresia (Kekulé No. 69), married name Bechinie. She appears in the records under several names. In the 1768 baptismal entry from Amschelberg she is first recorded as Apollonia Jablotzkin — a name carried over from her first husband Abraham Jablonsky — and at the baptism receives the name Theresia with the byname Kosso-Horska. The earliest primary-source family name pointing to her own family of origin appears one year later: in the marriage register of October 22, 1769 she is entered as "Rudolphiana from Kosova Hora". Her maiden name "Kopidlansky" only surfaces much later — in 1803 the marriage entry of her son Josef explicitly names her "Theresia née Kopidlansky from Kosso Hora, alias Amselberg". In the consistorial act of the Archbishop's Office of 1768 she additionally appears as Peol, daughter of a Moshe — the only primary-source reference to her Jewish family of origin. To be distinguished from this is the designation "Pessel of Miskowitz", which the Jewish genealogist Julius Müller communicated to me by email without source citation; whether "Peol" and "Pessel" refer to the same person is plausible, but not documented. The surviving parish register entries confirm that she was of Jewish origin, converted to Christianity in 1768 in Amschelberg (Kosova Hora), and in 1769 married the huntsman Philipp Bechinie.

This is exactly where DNA comes in. Among my matches, one particularly strong hit stood out: the Zeisl family from Vienna — a Jewish family with roots in the same Bohemian region. The following chapters document the systematic analysis of this discovery — from the DNA results through the family trees to the open questions.

1. Background — The Zeisl Match

On GEDmatch, Wolfgang Pilz and Walter Steven Z. share 43 common matches. A single segment of 29.1 cM on Chromosome 9 confirms a real genealogical connection — not endogamy noise.

The Core Puzzle: How are the Pilz and Zeisl families related? According to MRCA calculation (4.3 generations, statistical mean with spread), the common ancestor likely lived between 1830 and 1870. The connection probably runs through relatives of Theresia who remained in Bohemia's Jewish marriage network after her conversion in 1768.

Y-DNA of the Zeisl Family — A Conversion Finding

Research by Zeisl descendants revealed another remarkable finding: Walter Steven Z.'s Y-DNA shows a match with Peter C. in Brno, whose line traces back to a Zeisel who was already Catholic by the mid-18th century. Since the Y-DNA line is otherwise consistent with a deep Jewish background, a conversion from Judaism to Christianity must have taken place at that time.

Significance for surname research: This finding proves that the surname Zeisel already existed as a Jewish name before the Tolerance Edict of Emperor Joseph II (1782) mandated the adoption of fixed surnames. A conversion in the mid-18th century falls in the same period as Theresia's conversion from Judaism to Catholicism in 1768 — two independent conversions in the same Bohemian-Moravian region.

The closest geographical reference point of the Zeisl family to Košová Hora was long considered to be Koloděje nad Lužnicí (cf. Places of Origin, Section 7: Stampfer/Lewy family). Since the discovery that a Zeisl family is also documented in Miskowitz (Myslkovice, ~45 km southeast), this location now takes precedence as the nearest reference point (cf. Places of Origin, Section 7).

Note on the place assignment Miskowitz/Myslkovice: The triangulations and the Lazarus pseudo-kits demonstrate that I share a common ancestor with Walter Steven Z. 5–8 generations back. The location of that ancestor cannot be read directly from the DNA. The assignment to Miskowitz/Myslkovice rests on historical indicators (Regina Stern *1786 Myslkovice, Seligmann Fürth *1784 Sušice, Zeisl line Rozsochy, Stern/Fürth/Amschelberg nexus). All these places lie within the South Bohemian triangle — so the region is plausible, but the specific location could still shift as new findings emerge. „Myslkovice ancestor" is a working hypothesis, not a proven place assignment.

7. Places of Origin of Zeisl Ancestors

The Familiants Laws (Familiantengesetze, 1726–1849) forced marriages between Jewish communities in Bohemia and Moravia. Family heads were allowed to marry only one child (usually the eldest son) in their ancestral community; other sons and daughters had to emigrate. This led to large-scale marriage networks.

The South Bohemia connections explain the proximity to Košová Hora.

10. Excursus: Hermine Dannhauser née Zeisl — Innsbruck

           

The author of this site lives in Innsbruck himself. This excursus is included here because a Zeisl branch was also based in Innsbruck until 1938 — a coincidence of place of residence, not a close genealogical link.

           

Hermine Dannhauser (née Zeisl, *4 Jan 1875 Jungbunzlau / Mladá Boleslav) lived for decades in Innsbruck, Anichstraße 13. Her husband Josef Dannhauser died in Innsbruck in 1934; the couple had two children, Philipine (*28 Mar 1903 Innsbruck) and Walter (*6 Aug 1905 Innsbruck).

After the November Pogrom of 1938 the family was forced out of Innsbruck and relocated to Vienna. The deportations proceeded from there: Hermine and Philipine on 9 April 1942 on Transport No. 16 to Izbica (transit ghetto, onward transport to Belzec/Sobibor) — date of death unknown, declared dead. Walter was deported on 12 May 1942 to Maly Trostinez near Minsk and murdered there.

In Innsbruck, Steine der Erinnerung (stones of remembrance) now commemorate Hermine and Philipine in front of Anichstraße 13.

           

The Dannhauser family was part of Innsbruck's Jewish community. Witness: Berta D. (98 years old) survived Kristallnacht on November 9, 1938.

           

Research Note: This connection is a coincidence (the researcher lives in Innsbruck), not directly genealogically connected to the Pilz-Zeisl line. However, it illustrates the fate of the Bohemian Jewish diaspora.

           

Source: Achrainer, novemberpogrom1938.at

13. Glossary

Brief explanations of technical terms used on this page — DNA analysis, platforms and genealogy. Listed alphabetically.

Expand glossary
ADSA
Autosomal DNA Segment Analyzer. Tool on DNAGedcom that collects segments from GEDmatch matches and displays them graphically.
Autosomal DNA
DNA on the 22 non-sex chromosomes. Inherited from both parents; allows detection of relationships across all lines up to roughly 5–7 generations back.
Bridge match
A match shared with two otherwise unconnected persons or clusters, suggesting a linking ancestral line.
cM (centiMorgan)
Unit of genetic length of a DNA segment. The more cM two people share, the closer the relationship.
Chromosome browser
Graphical view of on which chromosomes and at which positions two people share DNA segments.
Endogamy
Marriage within a genetically bounded population (e.g. Ashkenazi Jewish, isolated villages). Produces many small DNA matches that do not trace to a specific recent common ancestor.
Familiants / Familiants Laws
Habsburg laws (1726–1849) that permitted only one son (usually the eldest) of each Jewish family in Bohemia and Moravia to marry in the home community. Resulted in far-reaching marriage networks.
FTDNA (FamilyTreeDNA)
Testing company based in Houston, Texas. Offers autosomal, Y-DNA and mtDNA tests.
GEDmatch
Public platform on which DNA raw data from various providers can be uploaded and compared across providers.
Half-Match / Total Half-Match
Sum of all half-identical DNA shared between two people, in cM. Common overall relatedness indicator.
Haplogroup
Branch of the human family tree defined by specific mutations in Y-DNA (paternal line) or mtDNA (maternal line).
IBD (Identical by Descent)
A DNA segment two people share because it was inherited from a common ancestor — as opposed to a chance match from population frequency.
JewishGen / SIG
JewishGen is a research portal for Jewish genealogy. SIGs (Special Interest Groups) are regional working groups within it, e.g. the Austria-Czech SIG.
Kekulé number
Sequential numbering of a person's ancestors: self = 1, father = 2, mother = 3, paternal grandfather = 4, etc. Each ancestor has exactly one fixed number.
Kit / Kit number
Unique identifier of a DNA dataset on a platform, required to compare against others.
Consistorial act
Canon-law procedure, e.g. preceding a religious conversion or a marital separation. Conducted by the diocesan consistory.
Matriken (parish registers)
Church registers of baptisms, marriages and deaths. In Bohemia and Moravia kept mostly by the parishes until 1949.
MRCA (Most Recent Common Ancestor)
The most recent common ancestor of two persons. In DNA analysis estimated from shared cM — with considerable spread.
One-to-One / One-to-Many
GEDmatch comparison tools. One-to-One compares two specific kits directly; One-to-Many compares one kit against the whole database.
Patronymic
Byname formed from the father's name or origin (e.g. "Kopidlansky" from an ancestor from Kopidlno). Common before the adoption of fixed family names.
Segment
A contiguous stretch on a chromosome that two people share identically.
SNP (Single Nucleotide Polymorphism)
A single-base variant at a specific position in the genome. DNA tests read hundreds of thousands of SNPs. More SNPs in a segment mean a more reliable match.
Tier 1 Triangulation
Paid GEDmatch tool that lists, for a given kit, all triangulating match pairs and their shared segments in tabular form.
Triangulation
Procedure in which three or more people share the same DNA segment. Only through triangulation can a segment be confidently traced to a common ancestor.
