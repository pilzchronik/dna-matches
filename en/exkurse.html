Excursus & Glossary

Wolfgang Pilz · Supplements to the main finding

This page collects what supplements the main finding without carrying it. It gathers four parts: two excursuses — the connection to the family of composer Arnold Schönberg (through Barbara Zeisl) and the Innsbruck fate of Hermine Dannhauser née Zeisl — together with the documented research status and next steps, and the detailed glossary of technical terms.

Excursus: Connection to Arnold Schönberg

Erich Zeisl's daughter Barbara married Ronald Sch., son of composer Arnold Schönberg (1874–1951, founder of twelve-tone technique). Their son is E. Randol Schönberg, the internationally recognized attorney in the Klimt case ("Woman in Gold"). Randol Schönberg is active on Geni.com and co-founder of JewishGen Austria-Czech SIG.

Erich Zeisl (1905–1959): The composer emigrated in 1938, first to London, then to Los Angeles. His work Requiem Ebraico is a classic of Jewish exile music. Archive: Exilarte.

I wrote to E. Randol Schönberg, and he replied. Contact has therefore been established — nothing more at this stage.

Excursus: Hermine Dannhauser née Zeisl — Innsbruck

I live in Innsbruck myself. This excursus is included here because a Zeisl branch was also based in Innsbruck until 1938 — a coincidence of place of residence, not a close genealogical link.

Hermine Dannhauser (née Zeisl, *4 Jan 1875 Jungbunzlau / Mladá Boleslav) lived for decades in Innsbruck, Anichstraße 13. Her husband Josef Dannhauser died in Innsbruck in 1934; the couple had two children, Philipine (*28 Mar 1903 Innsbruck) and Walter (*6 Aug 1905 Innsbruck).

After the November Pogrom of 1938 the family was forced out of Innsbruck and relocated to Vienna. The deportations proceeded from there: Hermine and Philipine on 9 April 1942 on Transport No. 16 to Izbica (transit ghetto, onward transport to Belzec/Sobibor) — date of death unknown, declared dead. Walter was deported on 12 May 1942 to Maly Trostinez near Minsk and murdered there.

In Innsbruck, Steine der Erinnerung (stones of remembrance) now commemorate Hermine and Philipine in front of Anichstraße 13.

The Dannhauser family was part of Innsbruck's Jewish community. Witness: Berta D. (98 years old) survived Kristallnacht on November 9, 1938.

Research Note: This connection is a coincidence (I live in Innsbruck), not directly genealogically connected to the Pilz-Zeisl line. However, it illustrates the fate of the Bohemian Jewish diaspora.

Source: Achrainer, novemberpogrom1938.at

Glossary

Brief explanations of technical terms used on this page — DNA analysis, platforms and genealogy. Listed alphabetically.

Expand glossary
ADSA
Autosomal DNA Segment Analyzer. Tool on DNAGedcom that collects segments from GEDmatch matches and displays them graphically.
Autosomal DNA
DNA on the 22 non-sex chromosomes. Inherited from both parents; allows detection of relationships across all lines up to roughly 5–7 generations back.
Bridge match
A match shared with two otherwise unconnected persons or clusters, suggesting a linking ancestral line.
cM (centiMorgan)
Unit of genetic length of a DNA segment. The more cM two people share, the closer the relationship.
Chromosome browser
Graphical view of on which chromosomes and at which positions two people share DNA segments.
Endogamy
Marriage within a genetically bounded population (e.g. Ashkenazi Jewish, isolated villages). Produces many small DNA matches that do not trace to a specific recent common ancestor.
Familiants / Familiants Laws
Habsburg laws (1726–1849) that permitted only one son (usually the eldest) of each Jewish family in Bohemia and Moravia to marry in the home community. Resulted in far-reaching marriage networks.
FTDNA (FamilyTreeDNA)
Testing company based in Houston, Texas. Offers autosomal, Y-DNA and mtDNA tests.
GEDmatch
Public platform on which DNA raw data from various providers can be uploaded and compared across providers.
Genetic group / community
A regional origin group of the last ~300 years assigned by MyHeritage or Ancestry.
Half-Match / Total Half-Match
Sum of all half-identical DNA shared between two people, in cM. Common overall relatedness indicator.
Haplogroup
Branch of the human family tree defined by specific mutations in Y-DNA (paternal line) or mtDNA (maternal line).
IBD (Identical by Descent)
A DNA segment two people share because it was inherited from a common ancestor — as opposed to a chance match from population frequency.
JewishGen / SIG
JewishGen is a research portal for Jewish genealogy. SIGs (Special Interest Groups) are regional working groups within it, e.g. the Austria-Czech SIG.
Kekulé number
Sequential numbering of a person's ancestors: self = 1, father = 2, mother = 3, paternal grandfather = 4, etc. Each ancestor has exactly one fixed number.
Kit / Kit number
Unique identifier of a DNA dataset on a platform, required to compare against others.
Consistorial act
Canon-law procedure, e.g. preceding a religious conversion or a marital separation. Conducted by the diocesan consistory.
Lazarus
A GEDmatch tool that partially reconstructs the genome of a deceased ancestor from several living relatives.
Matriken (parish registers)
Church registers of baptisms, marriages and deaths. In Bohemia and Moravia kept mostly by the parishes until 1949.
MRCA (Most Recent Common Ancestor)
The most recent common ancestor of two persons. In DNA analysis estimated from shared cM — with considerable spread.
myOrigins
FTDNA's ethnicity estimate (here version 3, 90 reference populations).
One-to-One / One-to-Many
GEDmatch comparison tools. One-to-One compares two specific kits directly; One-to-Many compares one kit against the whole database.
Pale of Settlement
The settlement zone in the Russian Empire from which many Ashkenazi matches descend (Belarus/Ukraine/Lithuania).
Patronymic
Byname formed from the father's name or origin (e.g. "Kopidlansky" from an ancestor from Kopidlno). Common before the adoption of fixed family names.
Segment
A contiguous stretch on a chromosome that two people share identically.
SideView
Ancestry's automatic assignment of matches to the paternal or maternal side — demonstrably faulty for this kit.
SNP (Single Nucleotide Polymorphism)
A single-base variant at a specific position in the genome. DNA tests read hundreds of thousands of SNPs. More SNPs in a segment mean a more reliable match.
Tier 1 Triangulation
Paid GEDmatch tool that lists, for a given kit, all triangulating match pairs and their shared segments in tabular form.
Triangulation
Procedure in which three or more people share the same DNA segment. Only through triangulation can a segment be confidently traced to a common ancestor.
