The common ancestor with the Zeisl family probably does not lie with Theresia herself, but one or more generations earlier — with her parents or grandparents. Theresia herself withdrew from the Jewish marriage network through her conversion in 1768; the DNA line to Zeisl therefore continued through Jewish-remaining collateral lines from the same ancestral generation.

Confirmation from a second side: a Bechinie cousin (MyHeritage, June 2026)

The MyHeritage data received in June 2026 added a match that supports the Theresia strand from a second, independent side: Andreas Oliver Bechinie von Lazan (Germany), a descendant of the Bechinie line that emigrated to Hungary and custodian of the family archive bechinie-von-lazan.de. The common ancestor is Josef Bechinie (b. 1776) ∞ Marianna Handl — Josef being the son of Theresia née Pessel. The bridge runs through the sibling pair born in Samechov: Marie (b. 1813) (my line) and Franz (b. 1815 → Hungary) (the match's line). The match and I are therefore fourth cousins.

I keep two levels apart: the kinship runs through Josef (after the conversion) — hence the expected amount of shared DNA. The Jewish connections lie one generation higher, with Theresia née Pessel; this level explains the large Ashkenazi shared-match cloud the match carries. A complete review of his 432 shared matches (June 2026) confirmed a self-contained Ashkenazi cluster that does not touch the maternal-alpine strand (see Reider, section 6a).

7. Places of Origin of Zeisl Ancestors

The Familiants Laws (Familiantengesetze, 1726–1849) forced marriages between Jewish communities in Bohemia and Moravia. Family heads were allowed to marry only one child (usually the eldest son) in their ancestral community; other sons and daughters had to emigrate. This led to large-scale marriage networks.

Jewish community with ghetto (since late 17th c.), 37 Familiants, synagogue since 1770.
Significance: Miskowitz is the first location where the place of origin of a direct ancestor of the author and a residence of the Zeisl family geographically overlap:
  • Theresia (Kekulé 69, b. ~1749; recorded as aged 19 in the 1768 consistorial record) presumably originated from Miskowitz (per J. Müller, without source citation; the only primary-source residence before her second marriage is Kosova Hora) and married into Amschelberg/Košová Hora c. 1764.
  • Zeisl family documented in the 1869 census (house XVI) — first concrete evidence of a geographic overlap between Theresia's presumed place of origin and a Zeisl family residence.
  • Bloch family also documented in the same census (house 56) — the name appears in the Zeisl pedigree (Rosalia Bloch, *1852 Čkyně).

Geographic Pattern: Three clusters observable — Central/East Bohemia (Zeisl/Reichmann around Rozsochy, Pardubice), South Bohemia-West (Feitler/Bloch/Stampfer around Budweis, Rožmberk) and South Bohemia-East (Miskowitz near Tábor — presumed place of origin of Theresia (per Müller), and simultaneously residence of the Zeisl family in the 19th c.). The South Bohemia connections explain the proximity to Košová Hora.

New finding May 2026: Veselíčko convergence in the Tábor District. In the village Veselíčko u Milevska (Písek District) two independent tracks meet — to be distinguished from the homonymous Veselíčko u Počepic ~24 km to the north (a scattered settlement of ~10 house numbers, Příbram District):
